Project 1

Web Site Critique

An analysis of Sablétine's website, sabletine.com, by Jacob Rowe

  1. What was your first impression of this site?

    My immediate impression of Sablétine's website was its simplicity--black text on a gently off-white background, with a couple of fancy fonts and some images to provide splashes of color.

  2. How does the site organize the flow of information?

    The site keeps the company name in the top left, a link to their Shopify page in the top right, and then puts any informative text in paragraphs on the leftmost two-thirds of the page as one scrolls down.

  3. Does the site structure make sense, or does it confuse you? Why?

    The structure is pretty clear to me. All navigation links are up at the top where they are easily visible from any page at any time, and each page represents a key aspect of the bakery and its products.

  4. Does the site graphics/layout/multimedia enhance the site or detract from it? Why?

    I feel that the site's layout enhances it, as all information that a potential customer might like to find is accessible with exactly one click. The graphics, I feel, are a bit more of a mixed bag. Rather than display a slideshow of each possible image when selecting a bakery product, the site appears to randomly select one image from a pool to show each time the link for that particular product is clicked. Given that the images are all different sizes and aspect ratios, sometimes it hides the informative text that a site visitor might be looking for by pushing it offscreen. Also, if the bakery wanted to show off their products, I feel that a slideshow or collage would be more effective, and the randomness and varying size of the images detract from the user experience.

  5. How long did it take for the homepage to load? Was the load time reasonable?

    Load times were very short, as there is very little on the site to display in the first place; as such, I found the load times perfectly acceptable.

  6. Was the text color and size readable? If a background color or image was used, did it enhance or detract from the site?

    The text was black and thus easily legible against the slightly cream-colored background. I feel that the subtle background color only enhanced the site, as it keeps the eyes focused on the text and on the images of baked goods on offer.

  7. Did the site have a navigation bar or other navigation tool? Was the navigation done well?

    The site has a rudimentary navigation bar horizontally stretched across the top of the screen just under the business name and link to the Shopify page. The bar simply contains a link to a page for each type of baked goods on offer, and a single link with contact information. However, every single page has a footer with the bakery's address and phone number, so the contact page seems mostly redundant (or would perhaps be better renamed "Hours" or "Socials" to reflect its actual contents).

  8. Did the site have a color scheme or look and feel that was repeated on all the pages?

    Every page was identically formatted in color, font, and text layout, so yes, there was a consistent scheme.

  9. How does the site respond to smaller window sizes?

    Sablétine's website has empty whitespace sidebars or side borders that shrink and grow with changes in the aspect ratio of the browser window, but only to the point of reaching the actual page content. If the browser window is less wide than any of the content on any of their pages, it does not automatically adjust image or text sizing but instead reveals the dreaded horizontal scroll bar to allow a user to peruse the page only a small visual portion at a time. Likewise, the site reveals a vertical scroll bar if the browser window is not tall enough to show all content on a page.

  10. What does this site offer that was unusual or very well done?

    It seemed unusual to me (at least in comparison to most modern websites) that each link in the navigation bar was to an entirely separate page, rather than some sort of embedded page or drop-down information delivery system. I really liked the simplicity of just changing pages, and the site load times were fast enough that the average user would likely not notice that each click on the navigation bar loads an entirely separate site rather than some hidden or embedded content.
